## SECTION 1: Client Acquisition

### ACQ-01 — **Cold Outreach Email**

```
Write a cold outreach email to a potential client. Requirements:
- Under 150 words total
- No "I hope this finds you well" or similar filler openers
- Open with one specific observation about their business: [OBSERVATION — e.g., "I noticed your SaaS landing page positions you against enterprise tools, but your pricing page targets SMBs"]
- Name one pain point that observation reveals: [PAIN POINT]
- Include one proof point — a result I got for a similar client: [RESULT — e.g., "I helped a B2B SaaS company increase trial-to-paid conversion by 34% by fixing this exact mismatch"]
- End with a low-friction CTA — not "hop on a call," something like a yes/no question or a single link
- Sign off as: [YOUR NAME], [YOUR SPECIALTY]
- Do not use buzzwords like "synergy," "leverage," or "circle back"
- Tone: direct, confident, peer-to-peer — not vendor pitching to buyer
```

### ACQ-02 — **LinkedIn Connection Request**

```
Write a LinkedIn connection request message. Hard requirements:
- Under 300 characters (LinkedIn's limit)
- Do not say "I'd love to connect" or "I came across your profile"
- Reference something specific about their work or content: [SPECIFIC THING — e.g., "Your post about firing a client who paid late resonated — I've been thinking about that tradeoff"]
- Make it feel like one professional reaching out to another, not a sales opener
- No pitch, no ask, no offer — just a reason to connect that a real person would find genuine
- Their name: [NAME]
- Their role/industry: [ROLE/INDUSTRY]
- My specialty: [YOUR SPECIALTY]
```

### ACQ-03 — **Upwork Proposal Writer**

```
Write an Upwork proposal for this job posting. Follow this structure exactly:

Job posting summary: [PASTE JOB POSTING OR SUMMARIZE IT]
My relevant experience: [2-3 SENTENCES ABOUT YOUR BACKGROUND]
My best result for a similar project: [SPECIFIC RESULT WITH NUMBERS IF POSSIBLE]

Structure:
1. Hook (first 2 sentences): Address the specific pain they described in the posting — not a generic opener. Make them feel understood.
2. Proof (2-3 sentences): One concrete example of similar work with an outcome. Numbers preferred.
3. Approach (3 bullets): How I would tackle THIS project specifically — not a generic process.
4. CTA (1 sentence): A specific, low-pressure next step.

Total length: under 200 words
Tone: confident and specific, not eager or salesy
Do not start with "I" or "My name is"
Do not list your skills — show them instead
```

### ACQ-04 — **Case Study Writer**

```
Write a client case study I can use on my website and in proposals. Use this information:

Client industry: [INDUSTRY]
Client situation before working with me: [SITUATION — e.g., "They were running paid ads but had a 0.8% conversion rate on their landing page"]
The core challenge: [CHALLENGE]
What I did: [YOUR SOLUTION/WORK]
Results achieved: [RESULTS — include numbers, timeframes, percentages wherever possible]
Optional client quote or feedback: [QUOTE IF YOU HAVE ONE, OR "none"]

Format:
- Headline: A results-focused title (not "Case Study: Client Name")
- The Situation (2-3 sentences)
- The Challenge (2-3 sentences)
- The Solution (3-5 sentences — what you did and why)
- The Results (bullet points with numbers)
- Client Quote (if provided, format it as a pull quote)
- Closing line that naturally leads toward "want results like this?"

Tone: professional but human — tell a story, don't just list facts
Do not use the client's real name unless I specify — use "[Client]" or their industry
```

```
Write a referral request message to send to a past or current client. Requirements:

Client name: [NAME]
Project we worked on: [PROJECT SUMMARY]
How long ago / current status: [e.g., "wrapped up 3 months ago" or "ongoing"]
```

## SECTION 2: Project Scoping & Proposals

### SCOPE-01 — **Project Proposal Writer**

```
Write a professional project proposal for a potential client. Use this information:

Client name / company: [NAME/COMPANY]
Project overview: [WHAT THEY NEED]
My proposed solution: [HOW I PLAN TO APPROACH IT]
Timeline: [YOUR ESTIMATED TIMELINE]
Investment (price): [YOUR PRICE OR RANGE]
Payment terms: [e.g., "50% upfront, 50% on delivery" or "net 15"]
Number of revision rounds included: [NUMBER]
What's explicitly NOT included: [EXCLUSIONS]

Structure the proposal with these sections:
1. Executive Summary (3-4 sentences — what problem this solves and what they get)
2. Scope of Work (bulleted list of specific deliverables)
3. What's Not Included (clear, non-defensive list)
4. Timeline (phases or milestones with dates/durations)
5. Investment (price, payment schedule, what happens if scope changes)
6. Revision Policy (how many rounds, what counts as a revision vs. a new request)
7. Next Steps (exactly what they need to do to say yes)

Tone: confident and professional — this is a peer-level document, not a plea
Do not use the word "passionate" or "excited to work with you"
```

### SCOPE-02 — **Scope Creep Blocker**

```
Write a professional response to a client who has asked for something outside our original agreement. This is NOT an apology — it is a calm, firm business communication.

Original project scope: [WHAT WAS AGREED]
What they've just asked for: [THE NEW REQUEST]
My price or rate for the new work: [YOUR RATE OR ESTIMATE — or "I'll provide an estimate"]

The response must:
- Acknowledge their request without dismissing it
- Clearly identify that this falls outside the original scope (reference the agreement naturally, not accusatorially)
- Frame the additional work as a positive option — something you're happy to take on as a separate engagement
- Include a specific next step (e.g., "I can send a quick add-on proposal — want me to do that?")
- NOT contain an apology for holding the boundary
- NOT sound passive-aggressive or cold
- Be under 150 words

Tone: friendly, professional, unbothered — this is just how business works
```

### SCOPE-03 — **Discovery Call Question List**

```
Generate a discovery call question list for a new potential client. Context:

My service: [WHAT YOU DO — e.g., "brand identity design for tech startups"]
Client context (what I know so far): [WHAT YOU KNOW ABOUT THEM]
My goal: understand their real problem, gauge fit, and surface budget without asking "what's your budget?"

Create exactly 10 questions organized in this order:
1-2: Situational questions (understand their current state)
3-4: Problem questions (uncover what's actually broken or frustrating)
5-6: Implication questions (help them feel the cost of not solving it)
7-8: Budget-surfacing questions (indirectly reveal what they're willing to invest — e.g., "Have you worked with a [specialist] before? What did that look like?")
9: Failed attempts (what have they tried that didn't work?)
10: Decision process (who else is involved in saying yes?)

Include a 1-sentence note after each question explaining why it works
Tone for questions: curious, conversational — not interrogative
```

```
- Revision policy (clear, unambiguous)
```

## SECTION 3: Client Communication

### COMM-01 — **Difficult Client Email**

```
Write a professional email response to a difficult client situation. I need to hold a boundary or correct a misunderstanding without damaging the relationship more than necessary.

Situation: [DESCRIBE WHAT HAPPENED — e.g., "Client is demanding a full refund after receiving all deliverables and requesting two rounds of revisions, claiming the work 'doesn't match their vision'"]
My position: [WHAT YOU BELIEVE IS TRUE AND FAIR]
What I want to achieve: [e.g., "decline the refund, offer a paid revision round, keep the door open"]
Our contract says: [RELEVANT CONTRACT CLAUSE IF ANY]

The email must:
- Open by acknowledging their concern (not agreeing with it)
- State my position clearly and once — not repeatedly
- Reference any relevant agreement or prior communication without being accusatory
- Offer a reasonable path forward if one exists
- Close in a way that leaves the relationship intact if they choose to accept
- NOT contain: "I'm sorry you feel that way," excessive apologies, or language that concedes fault I don't have
- NOT be aggressive or defensive — be matter-of-fact

Length: under 250 words
Tone: firm, calm, professional — an executive tone, not a defensive one
```

### COMM-02 — **Late Payment Follow-up Sequence**

```
Write a 3-email late payment follow-up sequence. Do not make these sound like debt collection notices — they should be professional escalations that protect the business relationship while making clear that payment is expected.

Client name: [NAME]
Invoice number: [NUMBER]
Invoice amount: [AMOUNT]
Invoice due date: [DATE]
My payment terms (from contract): [e.g., "Net 15"]
Late fee policy (if any): [e.g., "1.5% monthly after 30 days" or "none"]

Email 1 — Day 1 after due date:
- Friendly, assumes it slipped through
- Reattaches invoice
- One clear action: pay by [date]
- Under 75 words

Email 2 — Day 7 after due date:
- Warmer but firmer
- References the contract payment terms specifically
- Notes any late fee that has or will begin accruing
- Offers to discuss if there's an issue
- Under 100 words

Email 3 — Day 14 after due date:
- Final notice tone — professional, not emotional
- States clearly what happens next (pause work, collections, etc.) — pick what applies: [WHAT HAPPENS NEXT]
- Gives a final deadline
- Under 100 words

Subject lines: professional, not passive-aggressive (e.g., not "FINAL NOTICE!!!")
```

```
Write a project status update message to send to a client. Make it scannable, specific, and under 200 words.

- What's been completed: [LIST]

- Decisions or feedback needed from client: [LIST — or "none"]
```

### COMM-04 — **Negative Review Response**

```
Write a professional public response to a negative review. This response is not just for the reviewer — it's for every future client who reads it.

Platform: [e.g., Google, Upwork, Trustpilot, Clutch]
The review (paste or summarize): [REVIEW TEXT]
What actually happened (your side): [YOUR VERSION — be honest]
Is any part of the criticism valid? [YES / NO / PARTIALLY — explain]

The response must:
- Acknowledge the reviewer's experience without agreeing with inaccurate claims
- If the criticism is partially valid: own that part specifically, without over-apologizing
- If the criticism is unfair: gently provide factual context without being defensive
- Show future clients that you handle conflict professionally
- NOT contain sarcasm, defensiveness, or legal threats
- NOT be longer than 100 words

Tone: calm, credible, mature — you are writing for the audience, not arguing with the reviewer
End with something that signals you're open to resolution if appropriate
```

### COMM-05 — **Contract Red Flag Response**

```
Write a professional email requesting changes to problematic contract clauses. I have received a client contract that contains terms I cannot accept.

Red flag clauses to address: [LIST THE CLAUSES — e.g., "1. Unlimited revisions clause. 2. IP assignment includes all future work. 3. 90-day net payment terms."]
My suggested alternative language for each: [YOUR PREFERRED TERMS — or "suggest reasonable alternatives"]
My relationship with this client (new or existing): [NEW / EXISTING]
How important is this contract to me? [NICE TO HAVE / IMPORTANT / CRITICAL]

The email must:
- Open by expressing genuine interest in working together (if true)
- Frame the requested changes as standard professional practice, not personal demands
- Address each clause specifically — not as a vague "some concerns"
- Suggest revised language for each clause (or ask me to supply it)
- Signal that this is a negotiation, not an ultimatum — unless I specify otherwise
- Be under 250 words

Tone: collaborative and professional — a peer asking for fair terms, not a vendor being difficult
```

## SECTION 4: Pricing & Negotiation

### PRICE-01 — **Price Increase Announcement**

```
Write a price increase announcement to send to my client list. This message should be confident, not apologetic.

My current rate / pricing: [CURRENT RATE OR PACKAGE PRICE]
New rate / pricing: [NEW RATE OR PACKAGE PRICE]
Effective date for new pricing: [DATE]
Grandfather offer (if any): [e.g., "Existing clients can lock in current rate for 3 more months" — or "none"]
Reason (optional — only include if it strengthens the message): [OR "omit"]

The message must:
- Open with the announcement directly — no long preamble
- State the new rate and effective date clearly
- If grandfathering: present it as a benefit for loyal clients, with a clear deadline to act
- NOT contain: "I've been putting this off," "I know this is a lot," "I hate to do this," or any language that undermines the increase
- NOT over-explain or justify the increase at length — one sentence of context maximum
- Close with a clear next step

Length: under 150 words
Tone: confident, warm, professional — this is a business decision, not a confession
```

---

### PRICE-02 — **Negotiation Response**

```
Write a response to a client who has pushed back on my price or asked for a discount. I want to hold my rate while keeping the conversation open.

My quoted price: [YOUR PRICE]
What they said: [THEIR RESPONSE — e.g., "That's more than we budgeted. Can you do $X?"]
Their stated budget (if given): [BUDGET OR "not stated"]
Am I willing to adjust scope to meet their budget? [YES / NO]
If yes — what could I remove or reduce? [LIST OPTIONS]

The response must:
- NOT immediately drop the price
- Briefly reaffirm the value at the quoted price (1-2 sentences — not a sales pitch)
- If offering a scope alternative: present it as a legitimate option, not a consolation prize
- If not adjusting: hold the price gracefully and leave the door open
- NOT use phrases like "I understand budgets are tight" or "I'll make an exception this time"
- Be under 150 words

Tone: confident, unbothered, collaborative — the rate is what it is; here's how we might make it work
```

---

### PRICE-03 — **Value Justification**

```
Write a value justification for my pricing that I can use in proposals, sales conversations, or when a client questions my rate.

My service: [WHAT YOU DO]
My rate / price: [YOUR RATE OR PACKAGE PRICE]
What I need to justify it against: [e.g., "a cheaper competitor," "their internal option," "their budget objection," "a previous lower quote they received"]
A specific result I've delivered for a past client: [RESULT WITH NUMBERS IF POSSIBLE]
The cost or risk of the alternative (cheap option, DIY, etc.): [WHAT GOES WRONG IF THEY DON'T HIRE ME OR HIRE CHEAP]

Write a value justification that:
- Leads with the outcome/ROI, not the deliverable or hours
- Quantifies the cost of the alternative (bad hire, DIY time, lost revenue, etc.)
- Uses my past result as social proof
- Does not compare me to competitors by name
- Feels like a confident explanation, not a defensive argument
- Can be used as a paragraph in a proposal OR spoken naturally in a sales call
- Under 200 words

Tone: calm, factual, results-focused — let the numbers do the selling
```

## SECTION 5: Operations & Admin

### OPS-01 — **Invoice Description Writer**

```
Write professional invoice line item descriptions for my invoice. These should sound specific and worth the price — not vague or generic.

Project / client: [PROJECT OR CLIENT NAME]
Each deliverable to describe: [LIST EACH LINE ITEM — e.g., "1. Brand identity package. 2. Website copywriting. 3. Strategy session."]
Any specific details about what was delivered: [DETAILS FOR EACH — e.g., "Brand identity included logo suite (3 variations), color palette, typography system, and brand guidelines PDF"]

For each line item, write:
- A specific description (2-3 sentences) that references what was actually delivered
- Language that signals value — what this enables or what problem it solves
- No vague words like "services rendered," "consulting," or "project work"

Also write:
- A professional invoice memo / note (1-2 sentences) to include at the bottom of the invoice that thanks them and reinforces the relationship
- A subject line for the email sending the invoice

Tone: professional, specific, confident — every word should make the invoice feel worth it
```

### OPS-02 — **Project Retrospective**

```
Write a project retrospective document I can use internally and optionally share with the client.

Project: [PROJECT NAME]
Client: [CLIENT]
Duration: [START DATE to END DATE]
What we set out to accomplish: [ORIGINAL GOALS]
What was actually delivered: [FINAL DELIVERABLES]
What went well: [LIST]
What didn't go as planned: [LIST — be honest]
What I'd do differently: [LESSONS LEARNED]
Opportunities for continued work: [e.g., "Phase 2 of the website," "monthly retainer," "none identified"]

Format:
1. Project Summary (3-4 sentences)
2. Accomplishments (bulleted — specific, not generic)
3. What Worked (2-3 bullets)
4. What I'd Improve (2-3 bullets — honest but constructive)
5. Lessons for Next Time (actionable, first-person)
6. Opportunities (if any — frame as a natural next step, not a pitch)

If this is being shared with the client: soften "what I'd improve" to be collaborative, not self-critical
If internal only: be fully honest

Tone: professional, reflective, growth-oriented
```

### OPS-03 — **Testimonial Request**

```
Write a testimonial request message to send to a satisfied client. Make it easy for them to say yes and easy to write.

Client name: [NAME]
Project we completed: [PROJECT]
Specific outcome or result they mentioned: [e.g., "They said the new homepage increased their demo requests"]
Where I'll use the testimonial: [e.g., "website, proposals, LinkedIn"]
Their communication style (if known): [e.g., "very busy," "friendly and chatty," "formal"]

The message must:
- Open by referencing something specific about the project (not just "it was great working with you")
- Make the ask feel small — "5 minutes" framing
- Give them 3 specific questions to answer so they're not staring at a blank page:
  1. What was the situation before we worked together?
  2. What specifically did I deliver or what changed?
  3. What would you tell someone considering working with me?
- Offer to draft something for them to edit if they prefer
- Be under 150 words total

Tone: warm, genuine, low-pressure — a request from someone who earned it, not a form letter
```
